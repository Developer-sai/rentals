Abstract 
—Users must navigate multiple disconnected government datasets to 
understand Ireland’s housing crisis. In this paper, we introduce a novel 
conversational tool designed to answer everyday questions about local 
rental and property markets. The system relies on a somewhat non-
traditional Agentic Structured-Data Retrieval-Augmented Generation (ASD-
RAG) approach. The system eliminates vector similarity search entirely. 
Instead, user queries are routed through a five-stage LangGraph pipeline 
that runs deterministic Pandas logic directly against official datasets 
(from the RTB, PPR, and CSO). While a modular Large Language Model—like 
LLaMA 3.3-70B or Google Gemini—takes care of reasoning and 
classification, Python strictly handles all the math. The system achieves 
sub-3-second latency with dynamic visualizations and exact source 
citations. We also implemented strict domain guardrails to refuse or 
redirect off-topic questions. Based on our empirical evaluation covering 
seven categories of intent, we observed a 99.1% numerical accuracy rate. 
For context, an unaided LLM recorded only 38.2%. 
Index Terms—Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Multi-Agent Systems, 
LangGraph, Irish Housing Market, Conversational AI, FastAPI, Model-
Agnostic LLM, Structured Data Retrieval, Affordability Analytics 
I. Introduction 
The Irish rental market continues to face severe strain. According to Q4 
2024 figures from the RTB, the national average for new rents reached 
€1,680 per month. That represents a 5.5% annual increase, amounting to a 
78% rise since 2015 [1]. At the same time, the CSO reported an 8.7% jump 
in its December 2024 Residential Property Price Index, underscoring the 
ongoing pressure on buyers [12]. In Dublin, premium market rents are 
increasingly challenging to access on a median wage [2]. 
Such data is technically public. However, it sits scattered across 
separate platforms, each requiring a different level of technical 
literacy to parse. The Property Price Register provides raw, unaggregated 
transaction logs, while the CSO’s own portals demand familiarity with 
complex statistical dashboards. Navigating these disparate systems 
creates significant friction for the average renter or prospective buyer 
trying to cross-reference market prices against their personal income. 
Research by Hagedorn et al. [3] indicated that centralised platforms can 
reduce search times by 60–70%. Despite this proven need for accessible 
aggregation, a unified tool pairing Ireland’s official housing data with 
natural-language questioning has previously not existed. 
Large language models (LLMs) might seem like an obvious fix. But using 
one out of the box introduces serious risks. Models tend to invent 
numbers. Also, they lack any knowledge of events occurring after they 
were trained. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) was introduced by 
Lewis et al. [4] to fix exactly this by fetching external evidence to 
ground the model. Standard RAG setups rely on embedding similarity. This 
works fine for paragraphs of text, but performs poorly when dealing with 
tables where exact numeric filtering is the actual goal. Because of this 
limitation, we propose Agentic Structured-Data RAG (ASD-RAG). Here, we 
combine the reasoning capabilities of an LLM with deterministic data 
operations instead of guessing with vectors.

We present four main contributions: 
1. A Conversational Interface: A functioning natural-language system 
covering official Irish housing data from 2010 to 2024. 
2. Auditable Pipeline: A five-stage LangGraph system that explicitly 
separates intent, data retrieval, arithmetic, reasoning, and formatting. 
3. Empirical Benchmarks: Our internal evaluations cover end-to-end 
latency, classification effectiveness, and an ablation study targeting 
the ASD-RAG architecture. 
4. Decoupled Architecture: Agent logic is separated from the underlying 
LLM provider, allowing developers to switch between Gemini, LLaMA, or 
others seamlessly. 
Following this section, we review relevant literature. Section III 
details our system architecture, while Section IV outlines the 
experimental results. Finally, limitations and next steps are covered in 
Section V. 
II. Related Work 
A. Retrieval-Augmented Generation 
RAG was originally proposed by Lewis et al. [4] as a way to combine 
sequence-to-sequence models with an external index. It performed very 
well on standard QA benchmarks. That said, it relies heavily on the 
assumption that unstructured text can be fetched by similarity. When it 
comes to tabular filtering, this assumption breaks down. 
Gao et al. [5] highlighted structured-data retrieval as a complex, open 
problem. They pointed out that simply flattening tables into text ruins 
numerical reasoning. TableRAG [6] attempts to solve this by alternating 
between text retrieval and SQL. With our proposed system, we took this 
idea further by eliminating approximate retrieval entirely. Every piece 
of data is accessed via hard-coded Pandas operations, ensuring every 
numeric claim maps back to a specific spreadsheet row. 
B. Multi-Agent LLM Orchestration 
Workflows can be modeled as stateful directed graphs, which is exactly 
what LangGraph does [7]. Nodes act as distinct agents, passing shared 
state back and forth through edges. Single-pass prompting often struggles 
with complex logic. In contrast, graph-based structures allow us to build 
pipelines where stages can be independently audited. As Wang et al. [8] 
discovered, using specialized sub-agents usually leads to better 
performance on multi-step queries than treating the LLM as a monolith. We 
relied on these findings when designing our own agent decomposition 
strategy. 
Answering off-topic questions badly can erode user trust very quickly. 
Shneiderman’s framework for Human-Centred AI [9] suggests that these 
assistants need to support decision-making rather than act as a 
substitute for it. Transparency and tight focus are both required. To 
achieve this, the system implements a firm system-prompt guardrail inside 
the reasoning agent, blocking any input not related to mortgages, 
rentals, or housing policy. 
D. Irish Housing Data and Information Asymmetry 
Information asymmetry acts as a structural barrier in the housing market. 
Norris and Byrne [10] explained how eligible renters routinely miss out 
on support schemes simply because the required details are difficult to 
locate. Looking at European social housing, Scanlon et al. [11] 
documented similar issues, noting that centralized access platforms 
noticeably increased scheme participation. Our architecture was built to 
address exactly this gap. By fusing RTB, CSO, and PPR data, it offers a

E. Agentic AI for Structured-Data QA 
In their work on ReAct, Yao et al. demonstrated how interleaving tool 
calls with reasoning traces improves factual accuracy. The proposed ASD-
RAG model applies a similar concept to structured data. Instead of 
browsing the internet, our agents use Pandas operations to pull exact 
statistics. Furthermore, our architecture demonstrates that when LLMs are 
fed structured numbers, they provide much richer contextual explanations 
compared to when they are forced to recall figures from memory. 
III. System Architecture and Methodology 
A. Architectural Overview 
At its core, the backend operates as a FastAPI service resting on a 
single /api/chat endpoint. Incoming requests enter a compiled LangGraph 
state machine. This machine consists of five sequential nodes: Intent, 
Retrieval, Analysis, Reasoning, and Response. The output is a formatted 
JSON object carrying the answer, dynamic chart data, and the relevant 
sources. We serve the frontend as static files from this exact same 
process. 
Fig. 1. Architectural diagram of the LangGraph pipeline. Purple nodes 
indicate LLM agents; green nodes represent deterministic operations. 
The state graph compiles at startup and persists. A HousingState 
TypedDict holds all intermediate data. Because of this, both retrieval 
and analysis are completely deterministic for any given query. The LLM 
reasoning step is the only place variation is introduced. A normal round-
trip takes less than three seconds. 
B. Data Layer 
We rely on three primary government datasets to ground the system’s 
responses. The RTB rent index file is roughly 5.7 MB, tracking precise 
county-level and local electoral area rental histories from 2010 to 2024. 
Next, the Dublin subset of the Property Price Register (around 27 MB) 
logs individual property sale transactions, documenting the exact 
addresses, dates, and final execution prices. Finally, the CSO Master 
Dataset provides localized median earnings and county-level mean sale 
prices, serving as the foundational macroeconomic baseline that allows 
our affordability agent to accurately calculate price-to-earnings ratios 
and local housing cost burdens. 
To save memory, all files load lazily using Python’s lru_cache. Each CSV 
is read from disk only once. Upon loading, county names get title-cased 
and dates are parsed day-first. The data_manager exposes typed functions 
(such as get_rent_stats or compare_counties) that return dictionaries 
matching a fixed schema. Because of this strict schema, the underlying 
CSV files could easily be swapped out for a SQL database later without 
altering the agent logic. 
C. Intent Agent 
First up is the Intent Agent. Operating at a low temperature of 0.1, it 
calls the designated LLM API to classify the user’s goal into one of 
seven distinct categories, such as rent_query or affordability. We kept 
the temperature low because we need structural consistency, not creative 
writing. It also pulls out necessary entities like the year or target 
county. 
can normalize messy inputs automatically. If it happens to generate 
invalid JSON, a regex fallback assigns a default general_chat intent. 
This prevents the whole pipeline from crashing over a formatting glitch.

D. Retrieval Agent 
Once the intent is known, the Retrieval Agent triggers the proper 
data_manager calls. If someone asks for rent stats, it evaluates the 
primary numbers and then attempts to fetch a five-year trend using a 
try/except block. For single-county comparisons, the national average is 
grabbed automatically as a baseline. 
Notice that no vector embedding happens here. We rely entirely on Pandas 
boolean masks running across in-memory DataFrames. Every statistic passed 
to the LLM traces back to a specific row, meeting the auditability 
standards recommended by Norris and Byrne [10]. 
E. Analysis Agent 
The Analysis Agent takes the raw retrieval data and performs rigid, 
domain-specific macroeconomic calculations. Rent queries receive weekly 
cost breakdowns alongside calculated gross income requirements based on 
the standard 30% affordability rule, which posits that households should 
not allocate more than one-third of their gross income to secure adequate 
housing. House price questions trigger mortgage estimators, projecting 
monthly repayment schedules based on current prevailing interest rates, 
typical 30-year terms, and standard minimum deposit regulations. We 
calculate rolling multi-year trend trajectories directly to explicitly 
flag rapidly appreciating or depreciating local markets. Finally, we 
assign an overall affordability rating based on a five-tier scale tied 
tightly to corresponding local median earnings. 
These math operations bypass the LLM completely. We store the resulting 
summary inside HousingState, preparing it for the narrative reasoning 
step coming next. 
F. Reasoning Agent 
Running at a higher temperature of 0.4, the Reasoning Agent turns the 
calculated data into readable text. The prompt treats the LLM as an 
expert Irish housing analyst. We deliberately included instructions on 
Rent Pressure Zones and Housing Assistance Payments. Crucially, a hard 
guardrail instructs the model to refuse off-topic inquiries. 
Because the model receives pre-calculated math and strict context 
dictionaries, its job is simply to explain what the numbers mean rather 
than invent them. Responses are limited to roughly 300 words and heavily 
utilize bullet points to present information clearly. 
G. Response Agent 
Our final node, the Response Agent, is responsible for packaging the 
diverse intermediate outputs into a cohesive JSON payload ready for 
direct frontend ingestion. It carefully formats the narrative reasoning 
block, constructs dynamic Chart.js configuration objects—including 
setting correct geometric axes, responsive tooltips, and appropriate data 
labels based on the query context—flattens critical numerical metrics for 
immediate display, and strictly logs the exact dataset sources to 
guarantee traceability. We mapped specific chart types to specific 
analytical intents. For instance, multi-county comparisons might trigger 
a clean radar chart, whereas historical cost trends produce an intuitive 
multi-series line graph. We purposefully injected a small element of 
controlled randomization into the chart selection process to ensure the 
user interface remains visually engaging and does not feel monotonous 
over extended conversational sessions.

B. Intent Classification Accuracy 
Using a holdout set of 140 queries, we tested the Intent Agent’s 
precision. It achieved a 93.6% accuracy rate overall. The few errors that 
occurred were strictly misclassifications between adjacent topics, such 
as confusing a rent_query with an affordability question. Positively, it 
never mistook an off-topic question for a housing query. 
Entity extraction worked well too. County targeting hovered around 97.1%, 
while year extraction hit 98.6%. Bedroom extraction was slightly lower at 
94.3%, mostly failing when users used vague slang. 
C. Ablation: ASD-RAG vs. Direct LLM Generation 
We ran 50 factual queries against three system setups: direct LLM 
generation, ASD-RAG without the Analysis Agent, and the full pipeline. 
External annotators checked every generated figure against the actual CSV 
records.

The explicit constraints of our retrieval operations handle the heavy 
lifting for factual grounding. Adding the strict Python environments of 
the Analysis Agent closes the final accuracy gap. Without it, the LLM 
still occasionally fumbles basic mortgage arithmetic even when 
successfully holding the correct base property price in context. 
Furthermore, during our direct LLM tests, the models frequently suffered 
from localized semantic hallucination: for instance, confidently 
misapplying historical UK housing policy initiatives or outdated 
affordability schemes directly to the distinct Irish market. They also 
tended to conflate listing asking prices scraped from old web articles 
with actual recorded transaction prices from the PPR. These systemic 
failures powerfully underscore exactly why deterministic tabular 
retrieval mechanisms remain mandatory for reliable financial query 
resolution. 
D. Guardrail Effectiveness 
We evaluated the system against 40 purely off-topic queries covering 
sports, politics, and generic coding questions. The Reasoning Agent 
identified and refused 39 of them (97.5%). The single slip-up involved a 
question about "American mortgage rates." The model briefly attempted to 
answer before stopping. While system prompts defend against obvious 
domain breaks, they occasionally struggle with queries that run parallel 
to the intended topic. 
V. Conclusions and Future Work 
A. Summary 
The proposed architecture proves that combining Agentic RAG with 
deterministic tabular filters is highly effective for government data. 
Processing requests in under 3 seconds, the current pipeline managed 
93.6% classification accuracy and 99.1% factual reliability. Direct LLM 
approaches recorded only 38.2% accuracy in our empirical evaluation. 
Fusing strict Python math with natural language explanation creates a 
system that is both readable and auditable. 
B. Limitations 
There are several clear technical limitations restricting the current 
pipeline. First, the setup relies entirely on static, pre-downloaded CSV 
files, meaning any sudden macroeconomic market shifts or new government 
dataset releases won’t register within the intelligence system until the 
next manual codebase update. Second, to manage computational memory 
thresholds during prototyping, the property sale dataset strictly covers 
the Dublin municipality, thereby limiting deeper property price analysis 
to the capital matrix. Additionally, without persistent conversational
